# Off Piste Blog Writing Pipeline Foundation

This document defines how Off Piste should think about blog strategy, topical authority, writing quality, evidence, and publishing discipline.

This is a foundation document. It holds the durable principles that guide the pipeline over time. Article backlogs, keyword lists, and draft calendars belong in separate working documents.

## Purpose

The blog should help Off Piste become easier to trust, easier to understand, and easier to discover across search engines, AI answer engines, and human research journeys.

Every article should build defensible authority around the questions, problems, and decisions that make a strong client more likely to choose Off Piste.

The pipeline should produce content that:

- Demonstrates expertise through specificity.
- Connects design, search, brand, content, and technology to commercial outcomes.
- Helps serious buyers make better decisions before they speak to us.
- Gives search engines and AI systems clear, well-supported material to cite.
- Adds a distinctive perspective or level of evidence.

## Quality Bar

The pipeline should produce fewer, stronger pieces. Each article should earn its place by doing real work for the reader and the topical map.

Strong Off Piste content has:

- A specific buyer problem.
- A clear argument.
- Evidence that supports the argument.
- Interpretation that turns evidence into insight.
- Examples that make the idea concrete.
- Internal links that strengthen the wider body of expertise.
- A practical commercial consequence.

## Current Positioning Reality

The live site is moving toward a broader position: a strategic design and technology partner that helps ambitious businesses turn expertise into trust, clarity, growth, and revenue through websites, brands, content, and AI-enabled systems.

Some legacy site copy still carries narrower "small business" service-page framing. Blog strategy should help pull the site toward the newer, sharper position.

Before publishing major new content, check whether the post supports the current direction:

- Strategic design partner.
- Websites as commercial systems.
- Search and AI visibility as trust infrastructure.
- Brand as market clarity and buying confidence.
- Content as authority building.

## Audience

The blog should be written for people who are already serious enough to research.

Likely readers include:

- Founders and operators of growing businesses.
- Professional service firms trying to look as capable online as they are offline.
- Teams whose website, brand, or content has fallen behind the level of the business.
- Businesses that rely on trust, expertise, and considered buying decisions.
- Companies worried about being found, understood, or recommended in AI-assisted search.

These readers need clear thinking, useful frameworks, evidence, and practical judgement.

## Topic Selection Principles

Primary topics should come from the field of problems where Off Piste wants to be trusted. Services help monetise that authority, but the topical map should begin with buyer questions, market shifts, decision friction, and evidence.

A topic is worth building around when it meets most of these criteria:

- It connects to a commercial reason someone would hire Off Piste.
- It reflects a problem the buyer already feels before they know the solution.
- It gives us room to show judgement.
- It can be supported with credible sources, data, examples, or field experience.
- It can be explored from multiple angles without becoming repetitive.
- It is specific enough to build authority, but broad enough to sustain depth.
- It helps a reader understand why quality strategy, design, search, content, or systems matter.

A topic needs more than search volume. It needs commercial relevance, defensible insight, and enough depth to sustain authority.

Keyword research still matters. It should enter after the territory and buyer problem are clear. Use keyword research to understand demand, language, intent, prioritisation, and how people describe the problem in the wild.

The order is:

1. Define the authority territory.
2. Define the buyer problem.
3. Use keyword research to validate demand and language.
4. Map search intent.
5. Decide what evidence would make the article defensible.
6. Write the piece that best serves the reader and the topical map.

## Strategic Topic Territories

These are durable territories. Working article plans should live elsewhere.

### Search, AI Discovery, and Findability

This is the first topical territory to build.

The working frame is being found and trusted in AI-mediated search.

This territory covers how businesses are found, represented, cited, and evaluated across Google, local search, AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and other answer engines.

The emphasis is visibility through clarity, structure, authority, and evidence. This territory makes a business legible and trustworthy to both people and machines.

Potential sub-areas:

- Local search and Google Business Profile.
- AI search and answer-engine visibility.
- Structured content, schema, and machine-readable pages.
- Search intent and decision-stage content.
- Citation-worthy content and evidence-backed pages.
- How search behaviour is changing.

### AI, Trust, and Business Capability

This is the second topical territory to build.

The working frame is using AI to improve business capability without weakening trust.

This territory covers how businesses can use AI to improve capability, communication, customer experience, content, workflows, and decision support while protecting trust, accuracy, brand quality, and commercial judgement.

The emphasis is practical adoption through clear use cases, reliable systems, useful knowledge, human review, and buyer confidence. This territory makes AI a business design problem rather than a tool-choice problem.

Potential sub-areas:

- AI strategy and use case selection.
- AI and customer experience.
- AI content, voice, and expertise.
- AI workflow automation and internal systems.
- Knowledge, data, and business memory.
- AI governance, risk, and trust.
- AI-enabled websites and lead journeys.

### Trust, Proof, and Buyer Confidence

This territory covers what makes a buyer believe a business is credible before they make contact.

This is more interesting than generic conversion-rate advice because it sits at the intersection of design, brand, content, proof, and sales psychology.

Potential sub-areas:

- How buyers evaluate expertise online.
- Proof architecture: case studies, testimonials, metrics, process, credentials.
- Website clarity and trust signals.
- Decision friction and buyer hesitation.
- Pricing confidence and perceived value.
- How design shapes credibility before sales conversations.

### Brand as Commercial Infrastructure

This territory treats brand as a system for being understood, remembered, and chosen.

The strongest angle is commercial: how positioning, naming, visual identity, tone, proof, and consistency affect buyer confidence and revenue.

Potential sub-areas:

- Positioning and category clarity.
- Brand consistency across website, proposals, decks, and onboarding.
- Rebrands as business maturity events.
- Visual identity as a trust signal.
- Message clarity and differentiation.
- The cost of looking smaller, less mature, or less expert than the business really is.

### Websites as Revenue Systems

This territory covers websites as operating assets that connect positioning, content, UX, technical performance, search, conversion, and post-click trust.

The angle is systems thinking.

Potential sub-areas:

- Website structure for complex or high-trust services.
- How pages support the buyer journey.
- Performance, accessibility, and technical foundations.
- Conversion paths and qualification.
- Content architecture and internal linking.
- When a website is holding back growth.

## How Topical Maps Should Work

For each strategic territory, create a map with:

- One central pillar page or guide.
- A small set of durable subtopic clusters.
- Supporting articles that go deep on one specific question or decision.
- Clear internal links between the pillar, cluster articles, service pages, case studies, and relevant resources.
- A refresh rhythm for keeping high-value content current.

The map should answer:

- What does this territory help Off Piste become known for?
- What buyer problems does it clarify?
- What expertise do we have that competitors will struggle to replicate?
- What claims will require evidence?
- Which existing pages should this content support?
- Which existing pages need to be updated so the site tells a consistent story?

Start by defining the territory, the buyer decisions inside it, and the evidence needed to write with authority. Article titles come after that foundation.

## Authorship and Editorial Standard

The public author is Lara.

Drafts should sound like Lara has done the thinking, checked the evidence, and is explaining the commercial consequence to a serious operator. The voice is informed, direct, curious, and specific. It should have a strong point of view, while staying grounded in what can be shown, cited, or reasonably inferred.

Packy McCormick's Not Boring is a useful reference for energy and synthesis: strong narrative spine, useful data, specific examples, and clear extrapolation from evidence. The useful lesson is the work underneath the voice. The reader should feel that the article has done real thinking before asking for their attention.

The current Off Piste blog tone is a good base. Preserve its calm clarity and commercial judgement. Improve it by making every substantive piece more evidenced, more connected to a topical map, and more useful to someone making a real decision.

## Writing Rules That Must Be Enforced

These rules are editing requirements.

A draft should be rejected and revised when it contains:

- Contrast framing such as "not X, but Y", "this is not about X, it is about Y", or similar rhetorical pivots.
- Em dashes.
- Semicolons.
- Colon-led sentence breaks used as a stylistic crutch.
- Generic openings about the modern digital landscape, today's competitive market, or businesses needing to stand out.
- Claims that feel impressive without evidence.
- Advice that could apply to any agency, any industry, or any blog.
- Lists that replace thinking.
- Conclusions that collapse into a generic contact-us pitch.

Preferred sentence shape:

- One idea per sentence.
- Plain transitions.
- Specific nouns.
- Active verbs.
- Short paragraphs.
- Evidence before extrapolation.
- Commercial consequence after explanation.

Preferred article shape:

1. Start with the problem in the buyer's world.
2. Explain why the usual reading is incomplete.
3. Introduce evidence, data, examples, or observed patterns.
4. Interpret what the evidence means.
5. Connect the insight to the buyer's decision.
6. Show how the idea fits the wider topical map.
7. End with the natural resolution for the article. This might be a practical next step, a strategic implication, a decision framework, or a concise synthesis.

## Editorial Review Step

Every article should pass through a separate editorial review after drafting.

The editor should check:

- Does this sound like Lara?
- Is there a real argument?
- Does each section advance the argument?
- Are there unsupported claims?
- Are the cited data points interpreted properly?
- Are there places where a chart, table, diagram, or visual would make the idea easier to understand?
- Does the piece earn its place in the topical map?
- Does the article help a serious buyer think more clearly?
- Has the style enforcement pass removed contrast framing, em dashes, semicolons, and colon-led sentence breaks?
- Does the conclusion create momentum in Lara's voice?

The editor should rewrite weak sections directly. Editorial review should improve the draft, not merely annotate it.

## Evidence and Citation Standard

Every important factual claim should be supported when it materially affects the argument.

Citations make the article harder to dismiss as AI-generated slop and easier for readers, search engines, and AI systems to trust.

Each article should have an evidence plan before drafting. The evidence plan should identify:

- The core claim.
- The data points needed to support it.
- The primary sources that can support those data points.
- The places where Off Piste is making an interpretation from the data.
- Any claim that should be softened because the evidence is incomplete.

Use citations for:

- Search behaviour statistics.
- AI search adoption or traffic claims.
- Google documentation and policy claims.
- Accessibility and performance standards.
- Conversion, UX, or buyer-behaviour research.
- Industry benchmarks.
- Legal, regulatory, or platform-specific claims.
- Any surprising or quantitative statement.

Prefer primary sources:

- Google Search Central.
- W3C or WCAG documentation.
- Government and regulator publications.
- Platform documentation.
- Original research reports.
- Reputable analytics datasets.
- Academic or industry research where relevant.

Use secondary sources carefully:

- Use them for interpretation rather than core facts.
- Do not cite low-quality listicles.
- Do not cite a source just because it ranks.
- Trace repeated SEO statistics back to the original source before using them.

Citation rules:

- Link directly to the source.
- Use the most specific source URL available.
- Record access date for volatile data.
- Do not overclaim beyond what the source supports.
- If a source is weak, either replace it or soften the claim.
- If a claim relies on Off Piste's experience or judgement, label it that way.

## Data and Visual Standard

When a post relies on numbers, trends, comparisons, or sequences, consider whether it needs a custom graphic.

Use graphics for:

- Search behaviour trends.
- Changes in AI search, zero-click behaviour, or citation patterns.
- Before and after comparisons.
- Buyer journey models.
- Topical maps and internal-linking architecture.
- Evidence hierarchies.
- Process diagrams.
- Tables that would otherwise become dense paragraphs.

Graphics should clarify the argument.

Each graphic should have:

- A specific point.
- A cited source where the graphic uses external data.
- Alt text that explains the takeaway.
- A caption that states the implication.
- A clean filename and reusable asset location.

If a graphic uses Off Piste interpretation rather than a single source dataset, label it as a model or framework rather than data.

## Anti-Slop Checklist

Before drafting, ask:

- What does this piece add beyond a generic AI answer?
- What specific buyer decision does it help with?
- What evidence will support the main claims?
- What examples make it concrete?
- What internal pages should it strengthen?
- What would a sceptical reader challenge?
- Why should Off Piste be trusted on this topic?

Before publishing, check:

- The article has a clear argument.
- Claims are cited where needed.
- The article includes data interpretation where data is used.
- Any useful graphics have been created or deliberately deferred.
- The piece links to relevant internal pages naturally.
- The page has a distinct role beside existing articles.
- The title and description are specific.
- The conclusion resolves the article in a way that fits the argument.
- The post adds authority to a defined topical territory.

## Internal Linking Standard

Internal links should make the topical map visible to readers and crawlers.

Every article should usually link to:

- The most relevant service or capability page.
- The relevant pillar or hub page, once it exists.
- Related cluster articles.
- Relevant industry, location, or case-study pages where they genuinely help.

Links should use descriptive anchor text that explains why the destination matters.

The goal is to make the site feel like a connected body of expertise.

## Publishing Workflow

The publishing flow should be boring and repeatable:

1. Choose the topical territory and buyer problem.
2. Use keyword research to validate demand, language, and intent.
3. Define the claim, angle, and evidence requirements.
4. Gather sources before drafting.
5. Create an evidence plan.
6. Decide whether the post needs a graphic, table, diagram, or model.
7. Draft in Lara's voice.
8. Add citations and internal links.
9. Run the editorial review step.
10. Run the style enforcement pass for banned framing and punctuation.
11. Check for cannibalisation against existing posts.
12. Add the markdown file under `content/insights/`.
13. Run the sync/build process.
14. Confirm the post appears in the generated data and sitemap.
15. Commit to `main`.
16. Push `main`.
17. Fast-forward `deployed`.
18. After deploy, use Search Console for sitemap and key URL inspection checks when appropriate.

## Indexing Reality

The pipeline improves discoverability by ensuring:

- The page is crawlable.
- The page is indexable.
- The canonical URL is correct.
- The URL appears in `sitemap.xml`.
- The post is linked internally from relevant pages.
- The content is useful, specific, and distinct.

Google Search Console can be used to submit the sitemap and request inspection or recrawl of important URLs. Treat this as a request to Google, with indexing earned through content quality, crawlability, internal links, and site authority.

The Indexing API is intended for specific supported content types. The standard blog flow should rely on sitemap inclusion, internal linking, crawlability, and Search Console inspection where appropriate.

## Maintenance

Authority decays if content goes stale.

Each topic territory should have a review rhythm:

- Pillar or hub pages: review quarterly.
- High-performing posts: review when data, search behaviour, or platform guidance changes.
- Evergreen supporting posts: review at least annually.
- Posts with dated claims: update or remove stale references.
- Internal links: review whenever a new related article is published.

Refreshes should improve usefulness, accuracy, evidence, and internal links.

The maintenance pass should include two link directions:

- Add organic links from the refreshed article to newer related posts.
- Add organic links from newer or related posts back into older cornerstone content where the connection helps the reader.

Updated articles should record an `updatedDate` in frontmatter. Article pages should show the original publish date and a subtle updated date beside it. The updated date also supports `dateModified` in Article schema.
